Why apprenticeships alone won’t fix care workforce retention

Phil Tinsley · July 2026

Apprenticeships have become the sector’s go-to answer to the care workforce crisis. And they have real value — a structured route into care work, a nationally recognised qualification, a way to signal that the role is a profession worth entering.

But an apprenticeship gets someone through the door. It doesn’t keep them in the building.

The retention gap

The care sector’s workforce problem isn’t primarily a recruitment problem. Providers recruit — often constantly. The issue is that people leave at the same rate they arrive, and sometimes faster.

The average tenure for a frontline care worker in the UK is well under two years. That means organisations are spending time and money training people — including funding apprenticeships — and then watching that investment walk out the door.

An apprenticeship without a progression pathway is a 12-month countdown.

What people actually leave for

Exit interview data from care providers — where it exists — tends to show the same themes: feeling undervalued, no sense of where the role could lead, poor management, and pay that doesn’t reflect the demands of the work.

Qualifications don’t fix any of these. What fixes them is:

  • A visible internal progression route — from care worker to senior to team leader to coordinator — with clear expectations at each level
  • Management that’s been trained to manage, not just promoted because they were good at care
  • Pay progression that’s tied to skills and responsibility, not just time served
  • A culture where staff feel their observations and concerns are heard

Apprenticeships as part of a system

The organisations with the lowest turnover tend to treat apprenticeships as one component of a wider workforce strategy — not the strategy itself. The qualification opens the door. What keeps people is what happens after it.

If your organisation is investing in apprenticeships but still losing staff at the same rate, the question worth asking isn’t whether the apprenticeship is the right one. It’s what you’re offering the person once they’ve got it.
