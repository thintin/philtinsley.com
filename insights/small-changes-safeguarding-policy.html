Small changes, big impact: redesigning a safeguarding policy for the people who actually use it

Phil Tinsley · June 2026

A 19-page safeguarding policy landed on my desk. Comprehensive, well-structured, and almost entirely useless.

Not because it was wrong. It wasn’t. Every safeguarding obligation was covered. The language was appropriate. The legal framework was sound. But the provider — a supported living service for young people — was heading into an OFSTED inspection, and when I asked a support worker what she’d do if a resident disclosed abuse during a night shift, she looked at me and said: “I’d have to go find the binder.”

That’s not a policy problem. That’s a design problem.

The document was doing too many jobs

The 19-page policy was trying to be three things at once:

  • A formal record of the organisation’s safeguarding obligations and governance
  • A step-by-step guide for staff responding to a live concern
  • A training and learning reference for people new to safeguarding

Each of those purposes is legitimate. But combining them into one document meant none of them worked particularly well. The governance language muddied the practical steps. The training content added length that slowed down anyone under pressure.

One policy, split into three artefacts

The solution wasn’t to strip the policy down — that would have created genuine compliance gaps. It was to pull each purpose into its own document.

The policy — a concise, formal document setting out the organisation’s safeguarding responsibilities, governance structures, and compliance commitments. Suitable for inspection, for board review, for audit.

The procedure — a single-page flowchart and accompanying checklist covering exactly what a staff member should do when a concern arises. Designed to be read under pressure, in a rush, possibly at 2am. No jargon, clear decision points, escalation contacts front and centre.

The learning resource — a structured reference guide for induction and ongoing training. Scenarios, worked examples, the “why” behind the steps. Not something you read on shift, but something that means you don’t need to.

The result

The same compliance. The same coverage. A fraction of the cognitive load.

In the inspection, when the inspector asked the support team about safeguarding procedures, they could answer clearly — not because they’d memorised a document, but because the document they’d learned from was designed for learning, and the one they’d use on shift was designed for that moment.

Why this matters beyond safeguarding

I’ve seen the same pattern across care providers: a single document trying to serve multiple audiences and ending up serving none of them well. It happens with medication policies. With complaints procedures. With lone working guidance.

The question worth asking of any operational document isn’t just “is this compliant?” It’s “can the person who needs this actually use it, in the moment they need it?”

If the answer is no, compliance is the floor — not the ceiling.
