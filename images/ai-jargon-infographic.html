A Guide for Care Leaders

AI Jargon Explained

Nine terms you'll encounter when vendors, consultants or colleagues talk about AI — and what they actually mean in a care context.

01
AI

Software that mimics human judgement by finding patterns in data. In care, AI can assist with documentation, scheduling and flagging anomalies — but it doesn't understand context the way a person does. The tool is only as useful as the workflow around it.

02
LLM

Large Language Model — the engine behind ChatGPT, Claude and most AI writing tools. Trained on enormous amounts of text, it generates fluent, human-sounding output. Useful for drafting policies or summaries, but it can produce plausible-sounding errors. Always verify.

03
Agent

An AI that acts, not just responds. Rather than answering a question, an agent can retrieve information, make decisions and complete tasks autonomously — like pulling a care plan, summarising it and sending it on. This is where AI starts operating inside workflows independently.

04
Compute

The processing power that runs AI. For care providers, this determines whether a tool runs locally (on your hardware) or in the cloud — with direct implications for data security, GDPR compliance and what happens when connectivity fails.

05
Token

How AI processes text — in chunks roughly ¾ the size of a word. Tokens define what an AI can "see" at once (its context window) and are often what you're billed for. Long care records or policy documents may exceed a model's limit in a single session.

06
Hallucination

When AI produces confident, fluent — but factually wrong — output. In a care setting, this is why AI-drafted assessments, care notes or incident reports must always be reviewed before being filed or acted on. Fluency is not the same as accuracy.

07
Workflow

The sequence of steps that get work done. AI doesn't replace workflows — it plugs into them. Before adopting any AI tool, you need to understand your current process. Most failed AI implementations started without that understanding.

08
Training Data

The information an AI learned from. It determines what the model knows — and doesn't. A general-purpose AI has no knowledge of your organisation, your residents or your CQC history unless you explicitly provide that context in your prompts or system setup.

09
Oversight

The human checks that sit around AI use. In regulated care, oversight is non-negotiable. CQC expects evidenced, accountable decision-making. If AI assisted, you need to show how the output was reviewed. Oversight is what makes AI use defensible — and safe.
